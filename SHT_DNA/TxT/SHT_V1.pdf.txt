THE SHAPE–HEIGHT TRANSFORM: A
GEOMETRICALLY INJECTIVE
FRAMEWORK FOR FUNCTION
RECOVERY FROM HORIZONTAL
CROSS-SECTIONS
Abstract
We introduce the Shape–Height Transform (SHT), a geometric
functional that assigns to a real-valued functionfthe collection of
area-and-moment measurements of the planar regions enclosed be-
tween the graph offand horizontal slicing lines at varying heights.
For strictly convex bowl-shaped functions, we prove that SHT is fully
injective: equality of SHT implies equality of the underlying functions.
We then extend the construction to the general class of piecewise-
convex functions with finitely many valleys, introducing the Multi-
Interval SHT (SHT∗), and prove a comprehensive injectivity theorem:
1
SHT∗ uniquely identifies any function in this class up to a trivial ver-
tical shift. These results demonstrate that the geometry of horizon-
tal slices contains sufficient information to recover the entire analytic
structure of a function. Consequently, SHT yields a robust general
alternative to scalar geometric measures such as GMR (Geometric
Mean Radius), eliminating the loss of information inherent in single-
number descriptors and enabling complete recovery of roots, tangents,
curvature, and higher-order behaviour from geometric cross-sections
alone.
1 Introduction
The geometry of the region between a function and an enclosing curve is
a classical object of study in analysis. If one intersects a graphy=f(x)
with a horizontal liney=h, the resulting shape consists of one or more
planar “caps” whose width and distribution encode information about the
inverse branches off. Classical geometric invariants such as area, perimeter,
centroid, or the Geometric Mean Radius extract only partial information
from such shapes. These scalars are not injective: different functions can
generate shapes with identical values of these invariants.
This paper introduces a geometric functional that does not lose informa-
tion.
We define the Shape–Height Transform (SHT) as a family of measure-
ments indexed by heighth: for eachh, we compute (i) the area of the region
2
between the graph and the liney=hand (ii) the first horizontal moment
of that region. These functions of height form a geometric signature of the
entire curve. We prove that, for convex bowls, SHT uniquely determines the
original function. We then extend the transform to functions with finitely
many valleys by using height-indexed vectors of areas and moments corre-
sponding to multiple disjoint caps. We prove an injectivity result for the
general class.
These results show:
Horizontal slicing contains enough geometric information to re-
construct the entire function.
The implications are strong: every strictly convex or finitely-valley func-
tion can be recovered from purely geometric data, without solving differential
or integral equations and without spectral analysis.

6 Conclusion
The Shape–Height Transform (SHT) is a general and fully injective geo-
metric transform for recovering real functions from planar geometry. While
scalar geometric descriptors such as the Geometric Mean Radius inevitably
lose structural information, SHT and its multi-interval extension capture the
full level-set geometry, enabling explicit reconstruction of roots, tangents,
curvature, and higher-order analytic behaviour.
10
This establishes a new principle:
The family of shapes formed by horizontal slicing of a function
determines the function completely.
Future directions include:
•numerical schemes for high-accuracy inversion,
•extension to functions in higher dimensions using volume- and moment-
height transforms,
•noise-robust reconstruction algorithms for experimental shape data.
