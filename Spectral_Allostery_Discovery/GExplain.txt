Explanation of Spectral Roughness Figures (102D, 1AOI, 1CGP)
================================================================================

These figures illustrate longitudinal and statistical properties of the
spectral roughness (Ω) measured along DNA molecules using the Matrix-SHT
anaylsis. The three structures shown represent distinct biological states:

- 102D : canonical naked DNA (structural control)
- 1AOI : nucleosomal DNA (strong protein-mediated confinement)
- 1CGP : CAP–DNA complex (regulatory protein binding)

Each figure highlights a different aspect of DNA mechanical organization.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Figure 1: Longitudinal Spectral Roughness Profiles (Raw Ω)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This plot shows the raw spectral roughness Ω as a function of axial position h
along each DNA molecule.

Key observations:
- Canonical DNA (102D) exhibits low and relatively uniform Ω values.
- Nucleosomal DNA (1AOI) shows Ω values that are orders of magnatude higher,
  reflecting strong confinement and wrapping by histone proteins.
- The regulatory complex (1CGP) lies between these two extremes, with localized
  regions of elevated Ω.

Interpretation:
Spectral roughness captures the degree of transverse mass asymmetry. Protein
binding and wrapping dramatically amplify this asymmetry and introduce strong
spatial structure along the DNA.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Figure 2: Log-Scaled Spectral Roughness (Comparative)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The same longitudinal profiles are shown on a log10 scale.

Key observations:
- Log scaling reveals internal stucture within each complex that is not visable
  on a linear scale.
- 1CGP shows pronounced fluctuations and plateaus, indicating localized
  mechanical modulation.
- 102D remains relatively flat, consistent with a mechanically uniform duplex.

Interpretation:
Log scaling allows comparison across biological states spanning several orders
of magnitude in Ω, and justifies the use of logarithmic measures in downstream
analysis.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Figure 3: Normalized Mechanical Signal Profiles (Ω / ⟨Ω⟩)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Each Ω profile is normalized by its mean value, highlighting shape and spatial
organization rather than absolute magnitude.

Key observations:
- Nucleosomal DNA (1AOI) exhibits broad, sustained regions of elevated signal,
  suggesting long-range mechanical organziation.
- Regulatory DNA (1CGP) shows sharp, localized peaks, consistent with focused
  functional perturbations.
- Canonical DNA (102D) fluctuates near unity with minimal structure.

Interpretation:
After removing trivial scale differences, distinct patterns of mechanical signal
propagation remain, indicating that biological state affects not just strength
but spatial organization of mechanical features.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Figure 4: Distribution of Spectral Roughness Along DNA
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Histograms of log10(Ω) values sampled along each DNA molecule.

Key observations:
- 102D shows a narrow distribution, indicating mechanical uniformity.
- 1AOI is shifted to high Ω values, reflecting strong and persistent confinement.
- 1CGP exhibits a broader distribution, with increased variance.

Interpretation:
Functional and regulatory DNA complexes are associated with increased spectral
heterogeneity, not merely increased average roughness.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Figure 5: Mechanical Coherence Along DNA (Autocorrelation)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Autocorrelation of Ω along the axial coordinate, used as a measure of mechanical
coherence length.

Key observations:
- Canonical DNA loses corellation rapidly, indicating short-range mechanical
  coupling.
- Nucleosomal DNA retains correlation over much longer distances.
- 1CGP displays intermediate behavior.

Interpretation:
Protein binding appears to extend the spatial range over which mechanical signals
are correlated along the DNA backbone, consistent with enhanced elastic coupling
under constraint.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Figure 6: Spectral Heterogeneity — Mean vs Variance
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Each structure is represented by a single point showing the mean and variance of
log10(Ω) along its length.

Key observations:
- 102D: low mean, low variance (structural baseline).
- 1AOI: high mean, moderate variance (strong but relatively uniform confinement).
- 1CGP: moderate mean, high variance (localized functional heterogeneity).

Interpretation:
Regulatory function appears to correlate more strongly with spectral heterogeneity
(variance) than with absolute roughness magnitude alone.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
General Notes
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Axial positions are local to each structure and are not aligned to homologous
  biological landmarks.
- These figures are intened as illustrative examples, not statistical population
  summaries.
- The analysis emphasizes geometric and mechanical organization extracted from
  DNA coordinates alone, without using protein information.
