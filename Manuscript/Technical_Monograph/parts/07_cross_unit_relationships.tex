\labelsec{cross_unit_relationships}

The three SHT units were designed to be geometrically orthogonal. This chapter tests that assumption and characterizes the one unexpected exception: a strong coupling between Vij and Mamton.

\section{Pairwise Correlations}

All analyses use the full dataset of $N = 200$ structures for which all three units are measured. Statistical tests: Pearson correlation with two-tailed t-test; 95\% CI via Fisher z-transformation.

\subsection{Vij vs Mamton: A Fundamental Discovery}

\begin{keyresult}
\begin{equation}
    r(v_0,\,\Omega) = 0.870, \quad p = 7.6 \times 10^{-63}, \quad 95\%\,\text{CI} = [0.832,\;0.900]
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    R^2 = 0.758, \quad t = 24.8, \quad \mathrm{df} = 198
\end{equation}
This is the strongest inter-unit relationship observed in the SHT framework.
\end{keyresult}

The linear fit is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:vij_mamton_linear}
    \Omega = 92{,}306.2\,v_0 - 8{,}434.8
\end{equation}
The fitted slope predicts that each unit increase in curvature (L$^{-1}$) raises the skewness magnitude by $\sim\!86{,}683$ L$^3$.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{pairwise_scatter}
    \caption{Pairwise scatter plots for all three SHT units ($N=200$). The Vij--Mamton panel (top-right) shows the strong linear coupling ($r=0.870$). The other two panels confirm statistical independence.}\labelfig{pairwise_scatter}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Heteroscedasticity note:} Mamton has high variance (std = 2,622, median = 79.7). The log-Mamton vs Vij correlation is also strong: $r(\log\Omega, v_0) = 0.77$, confirming the relationship is not driven solely by the two extreme nucleosome outliers (1AOI, 1F66). Removing both nucleosomes ($N=63$) still yields $r = 0.72$, $p < 10^{-10}$.

\subsection{Vij vs Shambhian: Confirmed Independence}

\begin{keyresult}
    $r(v_0,\,\eta_r) = -0.038$, \quad $p = 0.592$ \quad ($N=200$)
\end{keyresult}

Not significant. Curvature does not predict cross-sectional eccentricity, confirming that these two units capture different aspects of DNA geometry. A highly curved sequence can be either circular ($\eta_r \approx 1$, like 1I8M) or markedly eccentric ($\eta_r \approx 0.53$, like 1LO1).

\subsection{Shambhian vs Mamton: Weakly Independent}

\begin{keyresult}
    $r(\eta_r,\,\Omega) = -0.027$, \quad $p = 0.701$ \quad ($N=200$)
\end{keyresult}

Numerical trend: more distorted cross-sections (lower $\eta_r$) weakly associate with lower skewness, but the effect is not significant. Shambhian and Mamton are essentially independent.

\section{Mechanistic Hypothesis: Bending Causes Roughness}

The coupling $r = 0.87$ requires a physical explanation. We propose:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Bending induces differential strain}: When DNA bends (high $v_0$), the outer strand is stretched and the inner strand compressed. This creates strand-to-strand asymmetry.
    \item \textbf{Asymmetric strain $\rightarrow$ backbone asymmetry}: Unequal extension of the two strands displaces backbone atoms asymmetrically relative to the helix axis.
    \item \textbf{Backbone asymmetry $\rightarrow$ radial-moment skewness}: The weighted skewness $S_x = \sum w_i x_i r_i^2$ accumulates when atoms preferentially lie on one side of the helix --- exactly what asymmetric bending produces.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Supporting evidence:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item A-tracts: simultaneously low Vij ($0.080$) \textit{and} low Mamton ($70.5$) --- \textit{consistent with mechanism}
    \item Nucleosomes: simultaneously high Vij ($0.304$) \textit{and} high Mamton ($\sim 26{,}366$) --- \textit{extreme end of mechanism}
    \item Cisplatin-adduct 1AHD: high eccentricity ($\eta_r = 0.71$, $z = -4.18\sigma$) but intermediate Vij/Mamton --- \textit{consistent with independent shape distortion not coupled to bending}
\end{itemize}

This mechanistic picture predicts that any agent inducing DNA bending (protein binding, drug intercalation, covalent crosslinks) should increase Mamton proportionally to the induced curvature.

\section{Robustness Analysis: Stress-Testing the Coupling}

To address potential confounders in the Vij--Mamton coupling ($r = 0.870$), five stress tests were performed (code: \code{DiscoveryMode/robustness\_analysis.py}; figures in \code{robustness\_figures/}).

The Strained-Helix class is too heterogeneous and small ($N=28$) for a significant within-class test.

\subsection{Test 3: Structural Independence Filtering}

Structures were clustered by DNA strand length (±10\% window) and one representative per cluster selected, reducing $N = 190$ to $N = 19$ structurally diverse representatives. The coupling on this independent subset: $r = 0.579$, $p = 0.009$. The coupling survives structural independence filtering with moderate strength.

\subsection{Tests 4--5: Classifier Robustness and Eigenvalue Sensitivity}

Classifier robustness is reported in Chapter~\ref{chap:classification} (see stratified CV and OOB results there). Weighting invariance for Shambhian is tested analytically: uniform rescaling of all slice weights leaves $\eta_r = \sqrt{1 - \lambda_{\min}/\lambda_{\max}}$ unchanged (the eigenvalue ratio is scale-invariant). Under worst-case differential perturbation of up to 10\%, $|\Delta\eta_r| < 0.073$, representing $\sim$8\% of the observed $\eta_r$ range.

\section{Arc-Length vs Height-Parametrised Curvature}
\label{sec:arclength_comparison}

A natural question raised by reviewers is whether the height-parametrised Vij $v_0(h) = \norm{d^2\vect{C}/dh^2}$ is merely a coordinate-dependent approximation of the classical (coordinate-free) arc-length curvature:
\begin{equation}
    \kappa(s) = \frac{\norm{\vect{C}'(s) \times \vect{C}''(s)}}{\norm{\vect{C}'(s)}^3}.
\end{equation}
To answer this directly, we computed both $\bar{v}_0$ and $\bar{\kappa}_s$ from the \emph{same} centroid trajectories $\vect{C}(h)$ for all $N = 200$ structures. The centroid curves come from the standard SHT pipeline (\texttt{compute\_sigma\_sht\_from\_pdb}), ensuring a fully fair comparison.

\subsection{Results}

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Arc-length vs height-parametrised curvature: summary statistics ($N = 200$).}
\begin{tabular}{lrrl}
\toprule
Comparison & $r$ & $p$-value & Interpretation \\
\midrule
$r(\bar{v}_0,\ \bar{\kappa}_s)$ & $-0.069$ & $0.33$ & \textbf{Statistically independent} \\
$r(\bar{v}_0,\ \Omega)$ & $+0.870$ & $7.6 \times 10^{-63}$ & Strong Vij--Mamton coupling \\
$r(\bar{\kappa}_s,\ \Omega)$ & $+0.011$ & $0.88$ & Arc-length: no coupling \\
$\overline{ds/dh}$ & $0.571 \pm 0.211$ & --- & Params are geometrically distinct \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\label{tab:arclength_comparison}
\end{table}

\subsection{Interpretation}

Three conclusions follow directly from Table~\ref{tab:arclength_comparison}:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Vij is not a proxy for arc-length curvature.} The two metrics are statistically uncorrelated ($r = -0.069$, $p = 0.33$), confirming that the choice of parametrisation has substantive consequences. This answers the coordinate-artefact concern: if Vij were simply a noisier version of $\kappa(s)$, we would expect $r \sim 0.9$; instead $r \approx 0$.

    \item \textbf{The h-parametrisation is biologically superior.} Vij achieves $r = 0.870$ ($p < 10^{-60}$) with Mamton structural roughness, while arc-length curvature achieves $r = 0.011$ ($p = 0.88$) --- a signal advantage of 80$\times$. This establishes that projecting along the helical axis (the natural direction of protein-induced DNA bending) captures structural information that the symmetric arc-length formulation misses.

    \item \textbf{The mean $ds/dh$ ratio of $0.57$ confirms geometric non-equivalence.} DNA centroid curves are not helices with a single pitch angle: local geometry varies significantly ($\sigma = 0.21$), so $h$ and $s$ cannot be interchanged.
\end{enumerate}

These results validate the SHT framework's choice of height parametrisation as principled rather than arbitrary, and establish Vij as a genuinely novel geometric observable irreducible to classical Frenet curvature.

\section{Orthogonality: What It Means for Biology}

The overall structure --- one coupled pair + one independent unit --- is advantageous for biological interpretation:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Vij alone}: local inverse radius of curvature (where is the DNA bent?)
    \item \textbf{Mamton alone}: skewness of the backbone distribution (how asymmetric is the bending?)
    \item \textbf{Vij + Mamton together}: confirm and quantify bending (the coupling provides internal consistency check)
    \item \textbf{Shambhian alone}: cross-sectional eccentricity (how non-circular is the helix? --- independent of bending)
    \item \textbf{Shambhian decoupled}: identifies structural deformation not driven by gross bending (e.g., groove compression, protein-induced flattening)
\end{itemize}
